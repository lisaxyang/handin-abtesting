About

This project is a practice in analyzing statistical data, categorical and quatitative, from two versions of a website.
In this project, a simple website was chosen to model an online cacti store. Two versions of the website were available to the public, with a 50% chance of loading either version after navigating to the URL. Data from a small sample was collected using logs of user interaction with the website.
The data were parsed and cleaned to look at 2 things: average time spent on the website (Time to Completion), and whether the user returned to the website or not after navigating to the cart page (Return Rate). A 2 Sample T-test and a Chi-squared test was conducted on the data. The conclusions are then determined from the results of these tests.
I hope you enjoy reading it; a lot of love was put into this piece. 😊

Hypotheses

Website Versions

For each website, I chose to emphasize a different aspect of the shopping experience.
In version A, I emphasized the process of purchasing. For each item, I put the price at the top of the section in very large font, and highlighted how many were already in the cart to the immediate right of the item. I also moved the cart to the top of the screen, so users could easily see how many items were in their cart also. In making these changes, I wanted the user to easily access and see how much they are paying and how many items they are paying for.
In version B, I emphasized the shopping experience. I increased the image size for all the items, increased the size of the "Add to Cart" button, sort of hid the prices, and placed the cart at the bottom of the page. In making these changes, I wanted the user to be more immersed in the items themselves and thinking about adding that item to their collection when they "add to cart", and divert attention away from prices and numbers.

Hypotheses for Each Metric 🧐

Metric 1: Time to Completion
Null Hypothesis: Version A will not lead to a faster time to completion than Version B.
Alternative Hypothesis: Versiona A will lead to a faster time to completion than Version B.
Reasoning: Version A will produce a more seamless experience with the user by having the important information (cost) easily visible to the average user that must work around a budget.

Metric 2: Return Rate
Null Hypothesis: Version A will not lead to a lower return rate than Version B.
Alternative Hypothesis: Version A will lead to a lower return rate than Version B.
Reasoning: Version A will lead to an experience that emphasizes the numbers first, so users will be more conscious of their purchases and less likely to need to go back once they reach the cart, because there should not be anything surprising to them once they reach it.
Aside: Personally, I have always found shopping websites that obscured the prices to feel almost distrustful. I would take longer to try to find the price, and also would wonder if making this purchase was reasonable if it feels like even the company would like you to forget how much it costs.

Data Collection

For this test, the link, which had an equal chance of dispalying either version A or version B once clicked, was distributed over two days to over 20 of my peers in the class. Without knowing which version they received, they played the part of a typical user and used the site to add $150 worth of cacti products to their cart.
The data was logged through Heroku, cleaned and organized by hand, and run through a Python script.
Aside: Having access to these logs also made me realize what kind of data that the websites I visit must have one me. As I'm sure many people are familiar with online shopping, especially during the pandemic, this made me realize what specific numbers industries may be storing to gain insight on what interfaces encourage their users to purchase, and to return because they had a relatively seamless experience the first time.

Conclusions

Takeaways

  • The sample size of A/B testing is very important in finding statistically significant results with small changes. After running this test, I feel like I have a better understanding of the advantage in resources that big industries have and how much harder it must be to collect data and improve your interface using A/B testing when there is low traffic to your site.
  • Using statistical tests allows for a controlled method of evaluating results. From the data, I would personally have guessed that version A had a lower return rate than version B, since only 1/3 of people returned to the main page in version A but 1/2 of people returned to the main page in version B. However, with such a small sample, the p-value was much larger than 0.05, and therefore I could not reject the null hypothesis.
  • A/B testing reveals how metadata can be incredibly revealing about users and their experience with a website. For example, just seeing that the same user returned to a webpage quickly after leaving, or how long it took them to make their first click on the page actually reveals more than normal people might expect. This data is incredibly useful and powerful given how trivial it may seem at first.
